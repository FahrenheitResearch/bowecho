The veri®cation set for each case was determined
through visual inspection of the radar data. After dis-
cussions with many operational and research meteor-
ologists, a set of veri®cation rules was developed. An
area of re¯ectivity was considered a storm cell if:
1) it had a maximum re¯ectivity of at least 30 dB Z,
2) it had a horizontal 30-dB Z area of at least 5 km
2 on
at least one elevation scan,
3) its estimated 3D mass-weighted centroid was sepa-
rated from other centroids by at least 5 km, and
4) the area was separated from other areas by a local
minimum at least 10 dB Z lower than the maximum
within the area.
Note that these rules do not include a criterion for depth.
Thus, if an area of re¯ectivity meets all of the above
criteria on at least one elevation scan, the area is con-
sidered a storm cell for veri®cation purposes. The SCIT
algorithm requires that an area of re¯ectivity meet size
and intensity criteria on at least two consecutive ele-
vation scans to be declared a storm cell. This algorithm
requirement is to avoid identi®cation of ground clutter
as storm cells; however, this can also cause shallow cells
to be missed. We will discuss the impact this has on the
performance results later.
Using NSSL 's Radar Algorithm and Display Software
(Sanger et al. 1995), radar meteorologists examined re-
¯ectivity data in accordance with the veri®cation rules.
Both composite re¯ectivity data and individual eleva-
tion scans were used to verify which areas of re¯ectivity
were cells. The 30-dB Z area and the horizontal location
of the 3D mass weighted centroid for each veri®cation
cell were estimated using the composite re¯ectivity
®eld. The 30-dB Z area was estimated using a circular
approximation with the radius of the estimated circle
being recorded for veri®cation. The maximum re¯ec-
tivity value, azimuth, and range were also recorded for
each storm cell. These characteristics constitute the ver-
i®cation set for each case. Both the SCIT algorithm and
the WSR-88D storm series algorithm were run using
the default algorithm settings (see appendix A for SCIT
settings). The horizontal location (azimuth and range)
of each algorithm-identi®ed storm cell was then com-
pared to the veri®cation dataset.
Figure 8 shows the method to determine hits, false
alarms, and misses. If an algorithm detection exists
within the radius of a human-veri®ed cell, the detection
is declared a ``hit.'' Conversely, if a veri®ed cell does
not have an associated algorithm detection, the algo-
rithm is assessed a ``miss.'' If an algorithm detection
exists outside of all veri®ed cells' radii, the detection
is declared a ``false alarm.''
